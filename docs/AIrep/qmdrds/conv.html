Feedback on Assignment: Strengths and Weaknesses

Strengths

1. Clear and relevant research question

  • The topic “Does higher education lead to higher salary?” is well-motivated and clearly connected to labor economics and relevant literature (e.g. Blaug, 1972).
  • The data choice (large Kaggle job-salary dataset) is appropriate for the question.

2. Good coverage of required econometric components

The assignment touches on all the requested elements:

  • Simple regression: You estimate a bivariate model (salary on education), present coefficients, p‑values, \(R^2\), and a prediction interval.
  • Multiple regression: You add experience and industry, interpret coefficients, compare with the simple model, and correctly discuss omitted-variable bias.
  • Dummy-variable model: You construct and interpret remote‑work dummies (( = , ), hybrid as base), and qualitatively discuss their effect.
  • Model comparison: You compute predicted salaries for specific profiles in different models and argue for a preferred model using \(R^2\) and interpretation.

This shows you understand the basic toolbox: coefficients, p‑values, \(R^2\), prediction, and dummy coding.

3. Interpretation of coefficients and significance

  • You consistently interpret intercepts and slopes in meaningful economic terms (e.g. “predicted yearly salary for lowest education” and “additional education decreases salary by 482.9”).
  • You correctly use the 5% significance level and recognize that very small p‑values imply strong statistical significance.
  • In the multiple regression, you identify that experience has a large positive effect and industry is not significant, and you explicitly connect that to the p‑values.

4. Awareness of model limitations

  • You notice the very low \(R^2\) in the simple regression and correctly conclude that education alone explains almost none of the variation in salary.
  • You question the negative coefficient on education and explicitly mention omitted variables such as work experience, skills, and industry.
  • In the multiple regression, you explicitly mention possible omitted variable bias in the simple model and recognize that including experience changes the education effect.

5. Use of prediction and intervals

  • You go beyond just presenting coefficients and actually use the model for:
    • Point predictions for several education levels.
    • A (approximate) 95% prediction interval for a specific education level.
  • This shows good understanding of how regression is used in practice, not merely as an estimation exercise.

6. Structure roughly follows assignment instructions

  • Sections 2, 3, 4, and 5 are clearly labelled in line with the task (simple regression, multiple regression, dummy analysis, model selection).
  • You also link to the script/estm.R and mention where the dataset is stored, which is good practice for reproducibility.

Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement

1. Conceptual issues: sign and coding of “education level”

Problem:
- You find that higher education is associated with lower salary, both in the simple and multiple models.
- You report “Education level = -482.9” in simple regression and “Education level = -463.991” in multiple regression.

Likely cause:
- You probably have reverse coding (e.g. 1 = highest education, 5 = lowest) or a non‑intuitive numeric coding of education. In that case, a negative coefficient is actually consistent with higher education increasing salary (because the number goes down as education goes up).

What’s missing:
- You never show or discuss how education is coded (which categories, which numbers).
- You therefore interpret the sign mechanically as “negative correlation”, which may be wrong if the coding is reversed.

Suggestion:
- Explicitly describe the encoding (e.g. 1 = PhD, 2 = Master, …, 5 = High school).
- Then re-interpret the sign correctly or, better: recode education so that higher values mean higher education, so a positive coefficient directly maps to your RQ.
- If you keep strange coding, show a small table mapping category → code and interpret accordingly.

2. Substantive interpretation: significance vs. effect size

You sometimes conflate statistical significance with economic importance:

  • Simple regression: p‑value is extremely small, so you say “there is a big significance between salary and education level”, but \(R^2 \approx 0.0003\); the effect is statistically clear but practically tiny.
  • Multiple regression: you call experience’s effect “extremely positive” without putting it in context (e.g. what is the typical salary or range?).

Suggestions:

  • Distinguish clearly between:
    • Statistical significance (p‑value < 0.05).
    • Magnitude / practical significance (is 5000 or 2700 large or small relative to average salary?).
  • When \(R^2\) is close to 0, explicitly state that “although the effect is statistically significant due to large \(n\), the model has almost no explanatory power.”

3. Prediction interval calculation is only approximate and not transparently justified

For the 95% prediction interval:

  • You take \(1.96 \cdot \text{SER}\) and simply add/subtract from the prediction: [ ,. ]
  • You do not:
    • Explain that this ignores the \((1 + h_0)\) factor from the exact formula.
    • State that you are ignoring estimation uncertainty in the slope and intercept.

This is acceptable as a rough approximation, but:

Suggestions:

  • Explicitly acknowledge: “We approximate the prediction interval using \(\hat y \pm 1.96\hat\sigma\), effectively assuming a large sample and ignoring the leverage term.”
  • Better: show the formula and, if using R, use predict(..., interval = "prediction") and report the exact interval.

4. Over‑reliance on \(R^2\) for model choice

In your conclusion you prefer model 2 largely because:

  • It has the highest \(R^2\) (19.2% vs. very low values in others).

This is incomplete:

  • \(R^2\) alone is not always the best criterion; adjusted \(R^2\) or AIC/BIC are often better for comparing models with different numbers of predictors.
  • The theoretical plausibility and interpretability of the model should be emphasized more than just \(R^2\).

Suggestions:

  • Mention at least one additional model selection criterion: adjusted \(R^2\), AIC, BIC, out-of-sample RMSE, etc.
  • Note that adding many weak variables can increase raw \(R^2\) but harm generalization.
  • Argue that model 2 is preferred because:
    • It includes theoretically important variables (education, experience, remote work etc.).
    • Coefficients make substantive sense.
    • It improves adjusted \(R^2\) compared to the simple model.

5. Incomplete description of variables and data cleaning

Throughout the assignment, some key points about the dataset and variables are missing or vague:

  • You mention 250,000 observations and 10 variables, but you do not provide:
    • A clear data description table (variable names, types, units, coding).
    • Any discussion of missing values, outliers, or transformations (are salaries in yearly units? Which currency? Log‑transform considered?).
  • For some variables (industry, remote work), we do not see:
    • How many categories they have.
    • Which one is the base category in regression (you mention hybrid as reference, but for industry you just say “not significant”).

Suggestions:

  • Add a small table in Section 1:
    • Variable, description, type (numeric, factor), codings and units.
  • Briefly describe any cleaning steps: dropped missing salaries? Filtered unrealistic values?
  • For categorical predictors, always state:
    • Reference category.
    • How many dummies were included.

6. Dummy variable section is underdeveloped

You do use remote‑work dummies but:

  • No regression table is shown (coefficients, SE, p-values, \(R^2\)).
  • You say “the model does not include interaction terms,” but the task explicitly allows/encourages interaction effects.

Suggestions:

  • Present the remote‑work regression output in a small table: intercept, Remote=YES, Remote=NO, their p‑values, \(R^2\).
  • Consider at least one interaction, e.g.:
    • Remote × Experience (does experience pay off more in remote jobs?),
    • Remote × Education (is the education premium different when working remotely?).
  • Interpret any interaction: explain how the effect of education or experience differs across remote/hybrid/on‑site groups.

7. Formatting, LaTeX, and minor technical issues

There are several small technical and presentational problems:

  • Spelling/grammar:
    • “higer salary”, “higer”, “significance is big”, “indusrty” etc.
    • Careless spelling weakens the professional impression.
  • LaTeX / formatting:
    • Expressions like $$6.45*10^-20$$ should be $6.45 \cdot 10^{-20}$.
    • In one place you write 14-5 which is unclear; probably –1.45 or similar.
    • Some formulas repeat words (“Salery”) and mix comma/point as decimal separators.
  • Word count code:
    • You define ford_count <- count_words_qmd("assignment_r.qmd") and then cat("Word count:", word_count), but word_count is never defined; this code will error.

Suggestions:

  • Run a spellchecker and grammar check on the entire text.
  • Standardize LaTeX to proper math notation and use a single decimal separator.
  • Fix or remove non‑working code chunks (or show the actual word count, not broken code).
  • Clean the Quarto structure: remove duplicated knitr::knit_exit() chunks; ensure YAML keys are correctly indented.

8. Lack of model diagnostics

The assignment does not discuss:

  • Residual plots,
  • Heteroskedasticity,
  • Nonlinearity (e.g. log-salary, squared experience),
  • Multicollinearity.

Given the large dataset, violations of classical assumptions are likely.

Suggestions:

  • Briefly inspect at least one diagnostic, e.g.:
    • Plot residuals vs fitted.
    • Histogram or QQ-plot of residuals.
  • Mention whether heteroskedasticity or nonlinearity appears to be a concern and, if so, suggest remedies (robust SEs, log-salary, polynomial terms).

Summary

Overall, the assignment successfully:

  • Answers the research question using multiple models,
  • Demonstrates understanding of regression coefficients, significance, prediction, and dummy variables,
  • Recognizes omitted-variable bias and the importance of including experience.

However, for a stronger econometrics mini‑thesis, you should:

  • Clarify and possibly recode key variables (especially education) to ensure the sign and interpretation make economic sense,
  • Separate statistical significance from economic importance,
  • Use more rigorous model diagnostics and selection criteria,
  • Develop the dummy-variable and interaction analysis more fully,
  • Improve clarity of data description, coding, and formatting.

Addressing these points would make the work more robust, transparent, and convincing both statistically and substantively.
